How India’s Plan of Democratizing Public Services Will Guide Other Countries and Break Monopolies

Over the last decade, India has built one of the world’s most ambitious experiments in digital public infrastructure. Instead of allowing essential services to be monopolised by a few private players, India chose open, interoperable platforms designed as public goods.

1. UPI: Democratization of Digital Payments

Unified Payments Interface (UPI) created a single interoperable payments layer that any bank or app could plug into, dramatically reducing costs and breaking closed payment monopolies.

2. ONDC: Democratization of E-Commerce

ONDC separates buyers and sellers from proprietary platforms, allowing small merchants to compete without being dependent on dominant intermediaries.

3. Aadhaar: Democratization of Identity

Aadhaar provided a universal digital identity that enabled efficient service delivery while reducing corruption and exclusion.

4. OCEN: Democratization of Credit

OCEN enables small-ticket loans using alternative data, opening formal credit access to millions of informal workers and MSMEs.

5. DigiLocker: Democratization of Documents

DigiLocker allows secure, paperless storage and verification of official documents, reducing bureaucracy and fraud.

6. ULIP & Logistics

India’s logistics digitalisation integrates rail, road, ports, and air cargo into a unified system, cutting costs and inefficiencies.

Conclusion

India’s digital public infrastructure shows that technology can be deployed as a public good. This model offers a scalable blueprint for countries seeking inclusive growth without monopolies.

7. iDEX: Democratization of Defence Innovation

The Innovations for Defence Excellence (iDEX) initiative opens India’s defence ecosystem to startups, MSMEs, and independent innovators. Traditionally, defence manufacturing was dominated by a small number of public sector units and large private contractors. iDEX breaks this monopoly by allowing new entrants to directly contribute to critical defence technologies.

8. GeM: Democratization of Government Procurement

Government e-Marketplace (GeM) digitizes and standardizes government procurement. By allowing MSMEs and startups to bid transparently for government contracts, GeM reduces corruption and prevents large suppliers from capturing procurement monopolies.

9. DBT: Democratization of Welfare Distribution

Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) ensures that subsidies reach beneficiaries directly through bank accounts. This reduces leakage, eliminates intermediaries, and strengthens financial inclusion across rural and urban India.

10. Democratization of Education

Online platforms such as NPTEL and IIT-led degree programs expand access to high-quality education beyond elite institutions. Students from remote regions can now access world-class learning resources without geographical constraints.

11. ONOS: Democratization of Academic Research

The One Nation One Subscription (ONOS) initiative aims to provide nationwide access to academic journals. This weakens publisher monopolies and accelerates research and innovation across universities and institutions.

12. ONOE: Democratization of Electoral Governance

One Nation One Election (ONOE) seeks to synchronize electoral cycles, reducing governance disruptions and election costs. The proposal aims to make democratic processes more efficient without compromising voter participation.

13. ABHA: Democratization of Health Data

Ayushman Bharat Health Account (ABHA) provides citizens with a unified digital health ID. It enables secure, interoperable access to medical records while empowering patients with control over their health data.

14. A Blueprint for the World

India’s digital public infrastructure demonstrates that technology can be designed as a public good rather than a monopolistic product. By prioritizing open protocols and interoperability, India provides a scalable governance blueprint for nations seeking inclusive digital transformation.
