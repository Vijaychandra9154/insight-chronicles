ULI: India's Attempt at a UPI Moment for Credit

2026-07-22

The Reserve Bank's Unified Lending Interface links land records, GST data and credit bureaus into one API layer for lenders — 64 institutions have joined so far, but two unglamorous bottlenecks are slowing it down.

UPI solved how money moves. ONDC is attempting to solve how commerce is discovered and transacted. Account Aggregators and OCEN built a consent-based rail for sharing financial data with lenders. The Unified Lending Interface (ULI) is the Reserve Bank of India's attempt to close that loop — plugging shared financial data, along with land records, GST filings and credit bureau scores, directly into a lender's own loan-decisioning systems through one standardised set of APIs. Where OCEN made it possible to build a lending marketplace on top of shared data, ULI is the plumbing the RBI wants every regulated lender to plug into by default.

1) A single pipe for a lender's messiest problem

ULI, built by the Reserve Bank Innovation Hub (RBIH) in Bengaluru and piloted from August 2023, is not a lending product or a consumer-facing app. It is an interoperable API layer sitting between a regulated lender and the scattered data sources a credit decision actually depends on: land records held by state revenue departments, GST returns, income-tax filings, banking data through the Account Aggregator network, dairy-cooperative records for milk-based income, and traditional credit bureau scores.

Before ULI, a bank lending against a farmer's land needed a bespoke integration with that state's land-records system — many of which are not fully digitised to begin with. ULI's pitch is standardisation: one set of APIs and one consent flow, so any participating lender can pull verified data from any connected source in close to real time, instead of every institution separately negotiating access.

2) The fourth rail in a familiar pattern

UPI standardised payments. ONDC is attempting to unbundle commerce discovery from any single platform. Account Aggregators and OCEN built consent-based data sharing and a lending protocol on top of it. ULI extends that logic one layer further: instead of just moving data between institutions, it standardises how a lender's underwriting systems consume that data alongside government-held records — land, tax, cooperative — that were never part of the formal financial system's data pool before.

It is the same institutional instinct behind the rest of India Stack: build a public API layer, let banks, NBFCs and fintechs compete on top of it, and avoid any single app or vendor owning the interface itself.

3) The numbers so far

As of 12 December 2025, 64 lenders — 41 banks and 23 non-banking financial companies — were onboarded to ULI, up from 36 a year earlier, according to figures reported by Medianama in January 2026. Data services connected to the platform grew from roughly 50 to more than 136 over the same period, spanning 12 distinct loan "journeys." The flagship ones are Kisan Credit Card renewals, dairy-loan disbursement based on cooperative milk-pooling records, and MSME loans underwritten on GST and banking data rather than collateral.

Early pilot participants included Axis Bank, Federal Bank, Union Bank of India and lending-tech firm FinBox. By January 2026, the Department of Financial Services had convened a separate push to bring urban co-operative banks — including SVC Co-operative Bank, TJSB Sahakari Bank and Janata Sahakari Bank (Pune) — onto ULI, echoing how UPI's later growth came from expanding beyond the first wave of large banks.

4) Where scale-up is actually stalling

Two structural bottlenecks recur in reporting on ULI's rollout through 2025. First, large banks with existing proprietary lending stacks and fintech partnerships have limited incentive to re-plumb around a shared standard that duplicates systems already working for them — a version of the same private-rail-versus-public-rail tension that slowed early UPI adoption among bigger players. Second, land records in many states remain incomplete or not digitised to a standard ULI can consume, which directly undercuts one of its headline use cases: collateral-backed agricultural credit.

5) The risk the RBI itself has flagged

Standardising data access does not, on its own, make an underwriting decision safer. The RBI has acknowledged that the platform's early emphasis on small-ticket personal loans drew scrutiny as delinquencies in that category rose. Faster data assembly can make weak underwriting faster too, not only good underwriting — a distinction that matters more as the network scales into Kisan Credit Card and MSME lending, where borrowers are typically more collateral-poor and more exposed to income shocks than the urban personal-loan segment ULI first tested on.

6) Who is accountable when something goes wrong

In a response to an RTI request, the RBI stated that it does not itself manage consent boundaries, data-use limits, or borrower grievances at the platform level — that responsibility sits with the individual regulated lenders and data providers connected to ULI, not with a central grievance-redress body for the network as a whole. For a system explicitly designed to move sensitive land, income and credit data across institutions at scale, that is a meaningful gap. It puts ULI in a familiar position: fast institutional rollout, with the accountability architecture still catching up.

ULI's own numbers suggest the platform has crossed from pilot into something closer to durable infrastructure — 64 lenders and 136 data services is a meaningfully different scale than the 2023 trial. Whether it becomes as consequential as UPI probably depends less on further lender onboarding and more on two slower-moving inputs no API can standardise away: how fast states actually digitise land records, and whether the grievance and accountability layer catches up before — rather than after — lending volumes running through it get large enough for a bad underwriting call to matter at scale.
