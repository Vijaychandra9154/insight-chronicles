Account Aggregators and OCEN: India's Third Rail After UPI and ONDC

2026-07-16

After building open rails for payments and commerce, India built one for financial data and credit — the Account Aggregator framework and the Open Credit Enablement Network.

UPI solved how money moves. ONDC is attempting to solve how commerce is discovered and transacted. There's a third, less visible layer that determines something arguably more consequential for economic mobility: who gets access to credit, and on what terms. India's answer to that problem is the Account Aggregator (AA) framework, paired with the Open Credit Enablement Network (OCEN) — together they form a rail for financial data and lending that follows the same open-infrastructure logic as the rest of the India Stack.

1) The problem: credit access without collateral or history

A large share of small businesses and individuals in India are effectively invisible to formal lenders — not because they're bad credit risks, but because banks have no clean, verifiable way to see their income and cash flow. A small shopkeeper might have years of consistent revenue running through a bank account, UPI transactions, and GST filings, but without a way to package and share that data with a lender, none of it counts as "credit history" in the traditional sense. The result is that formal credit skews toward people who already have collateral or a documented financial past, while everyone else is pushed toward informal, often expensive, lending.

2) The Account Aggregator framework: consent as infrastructure

The Account Aggregator system, licensed and regulated by the Reserve Bank of India, is built around a simple idea: a person or business should be able to consent, digitally and specifically, to share their financial data — bank statements, GST returns, tax filings, insurance and investment records — from one institution directly to another, without the data ever passing through the Account Aggregator itself. The AA is a consent broker, not a data warehouse; it never sees the underlying financial data. The user picks exactly what to share, with whom, for what purpose, and for how long, and that consent is logged and revocable.

This is the same design instinct that shows up elsewhere in Indian digital infrastructure — separate the pipe from the data, keep the individual in control of the exchange, and let the ecosystem of banks, NBFCs, and fintechs build on top of a shared, auditable consent layer rather than each negotiating bilateral data-sharing deals.

3) OCEN: turning shared data into a lending protocol

If the Account Aggregator framework is the pipe for financial data, the Open Credit Enablement Network is the protocol for turning that data into an actual loan. OCEN standardises how a Loan Service Provider — which could be an e-commerce platform, a payments app, or any business with a relationship to a borrower — can plug a borrower's request into multiple lenders' systems, using AA-sourced data to underwrite the loan quickly. The practical effect is that a business you already use for something else (a marketplace, an accounting app, a payments app) can become the front end for a working-capital loan, sourced from a bank or NBFC you may never interact with directly, underwritten largely on the strength of your real transaction history rather than collateral or a lengthy paper application.

4) What this changes for small businesses

For a small manufacturer, retailer, or service provider, the practical shift is speed and access: working-capital credit that used to require weeks of paperwork and often a personal guarantee can, in principle, be underwritten in days using verified cash-flow data the business already generates. For lenders, it lowers the cost of underwriting thin-file borrowers, because the alternative to AA/OCEN-style data sharing is either declining the loan outright or pricing it high enough to cover the uncertainty. Cheaper, faster underwriting doesn't guarantee cheaper loans for borrowers, but it removes one of the largest structural reasons formal credit has historically excluded them.

5) The open questions

Consent-based data sharing is only as good as how well ordinary users understand what they're consenting to — a real risk is that consent screens become another form of fine print people click through without reading. Adoption also depends on enough banks, NBFCs, and data-holding institutions actually joining the AA network as Financial Information Providers; a consent framework with too few participating institutions doesn't unlock much. And because OCEN and AA route sensitive financial data between private and public institutions, the security and governance of the consent layer itself has to hold up under real adversarial pressure, not just in a controlled pilot.

Why it matters

Put together, UPI, ONDC, and the AA/OCEN stack cover the three things a functioning market economy needs at scale: a way to move money, a way to discover and transact goods and services, and a way to extend credit based on real financial behaviour rather than proxies like collateral or a name on an approved list. Whether or not any single piece fully lives up to its design intent, the pattern is now a recognisable playbook — and other countries studying India's digital infrastructure are watching this credit layer as closely as they watched UPI.
